# KLARA Evidence Base: Most Heavily Weighted Sources

Ranked by frequency of citation and weight carried in core solution reasoning across sections A–H.

---

## 1. Nova Scotia Government — Annual Accountability Report on Emergency Departments (2023–24)
**URL:** https://novascotia.ca/dhw/publications/emergency-departments-accountability-report-2023-24.pdf  
**Type:** Government report  
**Confidence:** Confirmed  
**Used in sections:** A (ED volumes, LWBS, closures, wait times), C (capacity layer design), E (deflection model baseline), H (pilot recommendations)

The single most-cited primary source. Provides the definitive 2023–24 provincial ED dataset including 561,139 total visits, 10.02% LWBS rate, 51,552 hours of unplanned closures, facility-level wait times, and CTAS acuity breakdown. Nearly every quantitative claim in the access crisis framing traces back to this document.

## 2. CIHI — Primary and Virtual Care Access: ED Visits for Primary Care Conditions (December 2024)
**URL:** https://www.cihi.ca/en/primary-and-virtual-care-access-emergency-department-visits-for-primary-care-conditions  
**Type:** National health data authority  
**Confidence:** Confirmed  
**Used in sections:** A (15% CIHI benchmark, 9% virtual-appropriate estimate), C (non-diagnostic framing), E (cost differential), G (NACRS coverage gap)

Establishes the national baseline that NS is measured against: 1 in 7 Canadian ED visits are for conditions manageable in primary care, and 9% could have been handled virtually. Underpins the core argument that the navigation/routing gap is real and measurable at scale.

---

## 3. Nova Scotia Health — Need a Family Practice Registry Updates (2024–2026)
**URL:** https://www.nshealth.ca/clinics-programs-and-services/need-family-practice-registry  
**Type:** Provincial health authority — live registry  
**Confidence:** Confirmed  
**Used in sections:** A (unattached patient counts, trend), C (context layer design), H (pilot rationale)

Tracks the unattached patient population from ~160,000 (mid-2024 peak) down to 66,768 (January 2026). Central to the argument that a large, identifiable population segment is using ED and virtual care as substitutes for primary care — KLARA's primary target group.

## 4. McNaughton et al. (2024) — *Turbulence in the System: Higher Rates of LWBS Since 2020* — JACEP Open / PMC
**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11655912/  
**Type:** Peer-reviewed study  
**Confidence:** Confirmed  
**Used in sections:** A (LWBS adverse outcomes), D (safety framing), E (LWBS reduction value), G (safety risk argument for H1)

Documents that LWBS rates tripled post-pandemic and that LWBS patients face a 14% higher risk of death or hospitalization within 7 days. This converts LWBS from a satisfaction metric into a patient safety metric — the core reason LWBS reduction anchors the policy argument.

## 5. NS Health Physicians Portal — Nova Scotia Provider Digital Health Toolkit
**URL:** https://physicians.nshealth.ca/news/new-nova-scotia-provider-digital-health-toolkit  
**Sub-pages:** DIS & SHARE, OPOR, VirtualCareNS, Novari, AI Scribe Pilot, QHR Accuro, Pharmacare ESD  
**Type:** Official provincial ecosystem documentation  
**Confidence:** Confirmed  
**Used in sections:** B (all rows of ecosystem enhancement matrix), C (OPOR FHIR architecture), D (SaMD positioning)

The authoritative map of the existing NS digital health ecosystem. Every entry in the ecosystem enhancement matrix is grounded in this toolkit's documentation of what each service does, who uses it, and how it currently integrates. KLARA's "enhancement, not replacement" positioning depends on accurate understanding of existing capabilities documented here.

## 6. Implementation Science Communications — *Virtual Hallway Feasibility Evaluation* (2024) — BioMed Central
**URL:** https://implementationsciencecomms.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s43058-024-00651-3  
**Type:** Peer-reviewed feasibility study  
**Confidence:** Confirmed  
**Used in sections:** B (Virtual Hallway matrix row), C (architecture routing layer), H (Phase 2 integration rationale)

Documents that 84% of Virtual Hallway consults negate in-person specialist referral. Provides the clearest existing evidence that provider-to-provider digital tools produce measurable care pathway changes in Nova Scotia — a direct proof-of-concept analogue for what KLARA proposes at the patient-facing layer.

## 8. CMHA — State of Mental Health in Canada: Nova Scotia Profile (November 2024)
**URL:** https://cmha.ca/wp-content/uploads/2024/11/State-of-Mental-Health-profile-Nova-Scotia.pdf  
**Type:** National non-profit health data report  
**Confidence:** Confirmed  
**Used in sections:** A (MH burden statistics, budget allocation), B (811/mental health row), E (mental health navigation gap)

Establishes that NS spends only 4.9% of its health budget on mental health (below the 6.3% national figure) and that 28.8% of Nova Scotians report poor/fair mental health — above national average. Combined with the provincial wait times portal showing "Data unavailable" for MH&A adult services, this builds the case that mental health is KLARA's most underleveraged routing category.

## 9. Capra / Health Canada — SaMD Definition and Classification Guidance
**URL:** https://capra.ca/en/blog/software-as-a-medical-device-samd-definition-and-classification-2020-01-27.htm  
**Type:** Regulatory guidance interpretation  
**Confidence:** Confirmed (based on Health Canada MDR 2019)  
**Used in sections:** D (entire governance and non-diagnostic framing)

Provides the four exclusion criteria that position KLARA outside SaMD classification. This source is load-bearing for the entire governance section — the argument that KLARA is navigation infrastructure, not a regulated medical device, depends on correctly applying these criteria. Any drift toward diagnostic outputs would trigger Health Canada licensing requirements under Class II SaMD.

## 10. PMC — *Emergency Department Patients Who Leave Before Treatment Is Complete* (2021)
**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7972384/  
**Type:** Peer-reviewed systematic review  
**Confidence:** Confirmed  
**Used in sections:** A (LWBS return rates), E (LWBS reduction economic model)

Establishes that 33.5% of LWBS patients return to the ED within 30 days, and 11.5–23.2% require admission on return. These return-visit rates are used directly in the economic model to calculate the downstream cost value of reducing LWBS — converting a clinical metric into a budget argument.

**URL:** https://news.novascotia.ca/en/2024/07/03/province-expands-access-electronic-health-records  
**Type:** Government announcement

## 12. CBC Radio — *140,000 Nova Scotians Waiting for a Family Doctor* (2023)
**URL:** https://www.cbc.ca/radio/whitecoat/virtual-care-nova-scotia-doctor-waitlist-1.6982375  
**Type:** Journalism / expert interview  
**Confidence:** Approximate  
**Used in sections:** A (VirtualCareNS demand context), B (VirtualCareNS matrix row)

Documents that VirtualCareNS was turning away 100–150 patients daily at peak demand. This is the clearest evidence that the problem is not just ED overcrowding — even the designated virtual alternative is capacity-constrained, making KLARA's demand-sorting function essential to prevent flooding any single alternative channel.
